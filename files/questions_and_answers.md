## MODULE 2 — Operations on Vectors

**Q2.1** A mountain climbing expedition establishes a base camp and two intermediate camps, A and B. Camp A is 11,200 m east of and 3,200 m above base camp. Camp B is 8,400 m east of and 1,700 m higher than Camp A. Determine the displacement between base camp and Camp B.

**Q2.2** Find the dot product of 𝐴⃗ = 2𝑖̂ − 2𝑗̂ − 3𝑘̂ and 𝐵⃗ = −11𝑖̂ − 2𝑗̂ + 5𝑘̂.

**Q2.3 (Bonus practice)** Two soccer players kick a ball simultaneously from opposite sides. Red #3 kicks with 50 N of force while Blue #5 kicks with 63 N of force. What is the net force on the ball?

**Q2.4 (Bonus practice)** An airplane heads due north at 100 m/s through a 30 m/s cross wind blowing from the east to the west. Determine the resultant velocity of the airplane (relative to due north).

**Q2.5 (Bonus practice)** Find the sum of the two vectors: 𝑨 = 2𝒊 + 5𝒋 − 4𝒌 and 𝑩 = −2𝒊 − 3𝒋 − 5𝒌.

**Q2.6 (Bonus practice)** Find the magnitude and direction of the sum of the four vectors: 𝑨 = 𝒊+𝒋−6𝒌, 𝑩 = −2𝒊−4𝒋+10𝒌, 𝑪 = 2𝒊+2𝒋+6𝒌, 𝑫 = −11𝒊−7𝒋−7𝒌.

**Q2.7 (Bonus practice)** A 40 kg block of wood is pulled 100 m... *(see Module 6/7 for the numeric pulley/force problems)* — A car possesses vectors A (magnitude 53.0, pointing 30° N of E) and B (magnitude 34.0, pointing 63° W of N). What is the resultant vector, and its magnitude and direction?

### Answer Key & Explanations — Module 2

**2.1** Set up a coordinate system with east = +x, up = +y.
- Total east displacement = 11,200 + 8,400 = 19,600 m
- Total vertical displacement = 3,200 + 1,700 = 4,900 m
- Magnitude = √(19,600² + 4,900²) = √(384,160,000 + 24,010,000) = √408,170,000 ≈ **20,203 m (≈ 20.2 km)**
- Direction: θ = tan⁻¹(4,900/19,600) = tan⁻¹(0.25) ≈ **14.0° above the horizontal (east direction)**

**2.2** A·B = AₓBₓ + AᵧBᵧ + A𝑧B𝑧 = (2)(−11) + (−2)(−2) + (−3)(5) = −22 + 4 − 15 = **−33** (a scalar, as expected)

**2.3** Since the forces are opposite in direction: Net force = 63 N − 50 N = **13 N, in the direction of Blue #5's kick**

**2.4** This is a right-triangle vector addition (100 m/s north ⊥ 30 m/s west):
- Magnitude = √(100² + 30²) = √10,900 ≈ **104.4 m/s**
- Direction: θ = tan⁻¹(30/100) ≈ **16.7° west of north**

**2.5** Add component-wise: x: 2+(−2)=0; y: 5+(−3)=2; z: −4+(−5)=−9 → **Sum = ⟨0, 2, −9⟩**

**2.6** Add all x's, y's, z's:
- x: 1−2+2−11 = −10
- y: 1−4+2−7 = −8
- z: −6+10+6−7 = 3
- Sum vector = ⟨−10, −8, 3⟩ → Magnitude = √(100+64+9) = √173 ≈ **13.15 units**

**2.7 (bearing problem):** Convert bearings to standard angles (measured counterclockwise from +x/East):
- A: "30° N of E" → standard angle = 30°. Aₓ = 53cos30° ≈ 45.90, Aᵧ = 53sin30° ≈ 26.50
- B: "63° W of N" → standard angle = 90°+63° = 153°. Bₓ = 34cos153° ≈ −30.29, Bᵧ = 34sin153° ≈ 15.43
- Sum: x = 45.90−30.29 = 15.61; y = 26.50+15.43 = 41.93
- Magnitude = √(15.61²+41.93²) ≈ **44.7 units**; Direction ≈ tan⁻¹(41.93/15.61) ≈ **69.6° N of E**

**3.1**
- Total distance = 6 mi + 4 mi = **10 miles**
- Displacement = final position − initial position = 2 − 0 = **2 miles East**
- Average speed = distance/time = 10 mi / 6 s ≈ **1.67 mi/s**
- Average velocity = displacement/time = 2 mi / 6 s ≈ **0.33 mi/s East**

**3.2** Same direction → distance = displacement.
- Average speed = (7+2) km / (2+1) hr = 9/3 = **3 km/h**
- Average velocity = 9 km / 3 hr = **3 km/h** (same direction as travel)

**3.3**
- Distance = 7+2 = 9 km; time = 3 hr → Average speed = **3 km/h**
- Displacement = 7−2 = 5 km East; Average velocity = 5/3 ≈ **1.67 km/h East**

**3.4**
- Time south = 120/60 = 2 h; time east = 150/50 = 3 h; total time = 5 h
- Distance = 120+150 = 270 km → Average speed = 270/5 = **54 km/h**
- Displacement (resultant of 120 km south & 150 km east) = √(120²+150²) = √36,900 ≈ 192.1 km
- Average velocity = 192.1/5 ≈ **38.4 km/h**, direction ≈ tan⁻¹(120/150) ≈ **38.7° south of east**

**3.5**
- Distance = circumference = 2πr = 2π(0.5) ≈ 3.14 km → Average speed = 3.14/2 ≈ **1.57 km/h**

**3.6**
- Distance = 22+12+14 = 48 km, time = 0.5 h → Average speed = 48/0.5 = **96 km/h**
- Net south = 22−14 = 8 km; net west = 12 km → Displacement = √(8²+12²) = √208 ≈ 14.42 km
- Average velocity = 14.42/0.5 ≈ **28.8 km/h**, direction ≈ tan⁻¹(12/8) ≈ **56.3° west of south**

**3.7** v_f = v_o + at = 4 + (2)(3) = **10 m/s**

**3.8** Δx = v_o t + ½at² = 0 + ½(3.20)(32.8²) = 1.6 × 1,075.84 ≈ **1,721.3 m**

**3.9**
- a = (v_f−v_o)/t = (46.1−18.5)/2.47 ≈ **11.17 m/s²**
- Δx = [(v_f+v_o)/2]t = (64.6/2)(2.47) ≈ **79.8 m**

**3.10** Using v_f² = v_o² + 2aΔx → a = (7.10²−0)/(2×35.4) = 50.41/70.8 ≈ **0.712 m/s²**

**3.11**
- a = (0−12)/5 = **−2.4 m/s²**

**3.12** Δy = ½gt² → 8.75 = ½(9.8)t² → t² = 1.786 → **t ≈ 1.34 s**

**3.13**

**3.14** v_f = v_o + gt = 0 + (9.8)(2.45) ≈ **24.0 m/s** (downward)

**3.16**
- Since the trip up and down is symmetric, time to top = 6.50/2 = 3.25 s
- Initial velocity: v_o = g × t_up = 9.8 × 3.25 ≈ **31.85 m/s (upward)**

**3.17** At max height, v_f = 0: v_f² = v_o² + 2aΔy → 0 = v_o² − 2(9.8)(11.5) → v_o = √225.4 ≈ **15.0 m/s**

**3.18** At max height, v_f=0: 0 = 15² − 2(9.8)Δy → Δy = 225/19.6 ≈ **11.48 m**

**3.19** Let up be positive, ground = 0.
- Ball A (from 150 m, initial speed 10 m/s downward): y_A(t) = 150 − 10t − 4.9t²
- Ball B (from ground, initial speed v₀ upward): y_B(t) = v₀t − 4.9t²
- Both equal 50 m at the same time t. From Ball A: 50 = 150 − 10t − 4.9t² → 4.9t² + 10t − 100 = 0
- Quadratic formula: t = [−10 + √(100+1,960)]/9.8 = [−10+45.39]/9.8 ≈ **3.61 s**
- From Ball B: 50 = v₀(3.61) − 4.9(3.61)² → v₀ = (50 + 63.9)/3.61 ≈ **31.5 m/s (upward)**

**3.20** Slope of a position-time graph = Δx/Δt (rise over run). By definition, **average velocity = Δx/Δt** as well. As the time interval Δt shrinks toward zero, this slope becomes the slope of the tangent line at a point — which is exactly the definition of **instantaneous velocity**. Hence, the slope of a position vs. time graph *is* the object's velocity at that point.

**3.21–3.22** These require reading values directly off the specific position-time graphs shown in your slides. Method: 
- Instantaneous velocity at any time t = the **slope of the tangent line** to the curve at that point.
- The object's velocity is **negative** wherever the graph's slope is **negative** (position decreasing over time).
- The object is **at rest** wherever the graph is **flat/horizontal** (slope = 0).

## MODULE 4 — Motion in 2 Dimensions

**Q4.1** Consider a ball thrown upward with an angle of inclination of 90° with respect to the ground. What is the ball's change of position along the x-axis?

**Q4.2** Consider a ball dropped straight downward from the top of a building to the ground. What is the ball's change of position along the x-axis?

**Q4.3** Considering the figure given in class, which of the situations shown is **NOT** an example of free fall motion? *(Refer to the specific figure in your module material — answer depends on identifying which case involves a non-gravity-only force, such as air resistance or a powered engine.)*

### Answer Key & Explanations — Module 4

**4.1** At 90° (straight up), v₀ₓ = v₀cos(90°) = 0. Since there is no horizontal velocity component at all, the ball's **change in x-position is 0 (zero)** — it only moves vertically.

**4.2** A dropped ball has no horizontal velocity component (v₀ₓ = 0) at any time, since it falls straight down. **Change in x-position = 0 (zero)**.

**4.3** *(Conceptual — figure-dependent):* Free fall requires that gravity is the **only** force acting (no air resistance, no engine/propulsion, no other applied force). Any scenario in the figure showing an object with a powered or non-gravity force acting on it (e.g., a rocket firing its engines, or an object experiencing significant air resistance) would be the one that is **NOT** free fall.

## MODULE 5 — Newton's Laws of Motion

**Q5.1** Three professional wrestlers are fighting over a champion's belt. The figure shows the horizontal force each wrestler applies: F1 = 250 N (up and to the left, 53° from the horizontal reference), F2 = 50 N (purely horizontal, +x direction), and F3 = 120 N (purely vertical, −y direction). Find the x- and y-components of the net force on the belt, and its magnitude and direction.

**Q5.2** A net force of 15 N is exerted on an encyclopedia, causing it to accelerate at 5 m/s². Determine the mass of the encyclopedia.

**Q5.3** A sled is accelerating at 2 m/s². If the net force is **tripled** and the mass is **doubled**, what is the new acceleration of the sled?

**Q5.4** A sled is accelerating at 2 m/s². If the net force is **tripled** and the mass is **halved**, what is the new acceleration?

**Q5.5** Some kids want to know what happens when a person jumps off a chair to the right. Draw the FBD of the person and chair before and after jumping. What happens to the chair? Explain.

### Answer Key & Explanations — Module 5

**5.1** Using the given angles (F2 has zero y-component; F3 has zero x-component; F1 is at 53° from the horizontal):
- F1ₓ = −250cos53° ≈ −150.5 N (points left/negative x)
- F1ᵧ = +250sin53° ≈ +199.7 N
- F2ₓ = +50 N, F2ᵧ = 0
- F3ₓ = 0, F3ᵧ = −120 N
- **ΣFₓ = −150.5 + 50 + 0 = −100.5 N**
- **ΣFᵧ = 199.7 + 0 − 120 = 79.7 N**
- **Magnitude = √(100.5² + 79.7²) ≈ 128.2 N**
- **Direction:** tan⁻¹(79.7/100.5) ≈ 38.4° — since Fₓ is negative and Fᵧ is positive, the net force points **38.4° above the −x axis** (up and to the left).

**5.2** F = ma → m = F/a = 15/5 = **3 kg**

**5.3** New a = (3F)/(2m) = 1.5 × (F/m) = 1.5 × 2 = **3 m/s²**

**5.4** New a = (3F)/(0.5m) = 6 × (F/m) = 6 × 2 = **12 m/s²**

**5.5** *(Conceptual):* By **Newton's Third Law**, as the person pushes backward/downward against the chair to jump, the chair pushes back on the person with an equal and opposite force, propelling them forward/upward. In reaction, **the chair slides or is pushed in the opposite direction** the person jumps (e.g., if the person jumps to the right, the chair recoils to the left).

### Answer Key & Explanations — Module 6

**6.1** On a frictionless incline, a = g sinθ = 9.8 × sin34° ≈ 9.8 × 0.559 ≈ **5.48 m/s² (down the incline)**

**6.2** Since Normal force N = mg cosθ, a **larger** angle θ gives a **smaller** N (lower normal force). Since acceleration a = g sinθ, a **larger** angle θ gives a **larger** acceleration. **Relation: a = g sinθ — acceleration increases as θ increases.**

**6.3** *(Method only — angle not given in text):* a = g(sinθ − μₖcosθ), where θ is the incline angle from your class figure.

**6.4**
- Static (hanging still): T = mg = 50 × 9.8 = **490 N**
- Lifting upward at 2.3 m/s²: T = m(g+a) = 50(9.8+2.3) = **605 N**
- Descending at 0.75 m/s² (net downward acceleration): T = m(g−a) = 50(9.8−0.75) = **452.5 N**

**6.5** First, find the elevator's acceleration using v_f²=v_o²+2aΔx (taking downward motion as positive, decelerating to a stop):
0 = 10² + 2a(25) → a = −100/50 = −2 m/s² → this means **2 m/s² directed upward** (opposing the downward motion, i.e., decelerating it).
- (a) T − mg = ma (up positive) → T = m(g+a) = 800(9.8+2) = **9,440 N**
- (b) Passenger (50 kg) experiences the same acceleration. By Newton's 3rd Law, the force the floor exerts on the passenger (N) equals the force the passenger's feet exert on the floor: N = m(g+a) = 50(9.8+2) = **590 N**

**6.6** For m1 (on frictionless table, horizontal): **T = m1a**
For m2 (hanging): **m2g − T = m2a**
Adding both equations: m2g = (m1+m2)a → **a = m2g/(m1+m2)**
Substituting back: **T = m1m2g/(m1+m2)**

**6.7** For m1 on a frictionless incline (angle θ) connected to hanging m2:
- m2g − T = m2a (hanging mass)
- T − m1g sinθ = m1a (mass on incline, assuming it slides up)
- Adding: m2g − m1g sinθ = (m1+m2)a → **a = g(m2 − m1 sinθ)/(m1+m2)**
- **T = m1(a + g sinθ)**

**6.8** Compare the "pulling forces": m2g = 15×9.8 = 147 N vs. m1g sinθ = 18×9.8×sin30° = 88.2 N.
Since 147 N > 88.2 N, **the 15 kg mass descends, and the 18 kg mass slides up the incline.**
- a = (m2g − m1g sinθ)/(m1+m2) = (147−88.2)/33 = 58.8/33 ≈ **1.78 m/s²**
- T = m1a + m1g sinθ = 18(1.78) + 88.2 ≈ **120.3 N**

**6.9** First check direction: m2g = 6×9.8 = 58.8 N vs. m1g sinθ = 5×9.8×sin30° = 24.5 N → the 6 kg mass descends; 5 kg mass slides up the incline (friction opposes this, acting down-slope).
- Normal force: N = m1g cosθ = 5×9.8×cos30° ≈ 42.43 N
- Friction: f = μₖN = 0.2×42.43 ≈ 8.49 N
- m2g − m1g sinθ − f = (m1+m2)a → 58.8 − 24.5 − 8.49 = 11a → 25.81 = 11a → **a ≈ 2.35 m/s²**
- T = m2g − m2a = 58.8 − 6(2.35) ≈ **44.7 N**

**6.10** *(Figure-dependent — masses not given in text):* Method: for each block on its own incline, sum forces along the direction of the connecting string (T is the same throughout an ideal cord/pulley); set the net "pulling" side (heavier component along the incline) against the other, then a = (net force)/(total mass), same style as Q6.8.

**6.11 (Atwood Machine):**
- a = (m1−m2)g/(m1+m2) = (10−5)(9.8)/15 = 49/15 ≈ **3.27 m/s²**
- T = 2m1m2g/(m1+m2) = 2(10)(5)(9.8)/15 = 980/15 ≈ **65.3 N**
- (m1 = 10 kg descends; m2 = 5 kg rises)

**6.12**
- Fc = mv²/r = 945(10.0)²/25.0 = 945(100)/25 = **3,780 N** (friction force at maximum speed)
- μ = Fc/(mg) = 3,780/(945×9.8) = 3,780/9,261 ≈ **0.408**

**6.13**
- f_max = μmg = 0.850×945×9.8 ≈ 7,871.85 N
- v² = f_max·r/m = (7,871.85×35.0)/945 ≈ 291.55 → v = √291.55 ≈ **17.1 m/s**

**6.14 (top of loop):**
- a_c = v²/r = 4.00²/1.00 = **16 m/s² (toward center = downward at the top)**
- Fnet = ma_c = 1.50×16 = **24 N**

**6.15 (bottom of loop):**

- Fnet = ma_c = 1.50×36 = **54 N**
- At the bottom, tension acts toward the center (up), gravity acts away (down): T − mg = Fnet → T = 54 + 14.7 = **68.7 N**

**6.16**

**Q7.15** During a marathon, Jack and Jill go up a hill. Jack is twice as massive as Jill, yet Jill ascends the same distance in half the time. Compare their power output.

**Q7.16** A tired squirrel (mass ≈ 1 kg) does push-ups, elevating its center of mass by 5 cm to do 0.50 J of work in 2 seconds. Determine its power.

### Answer Key & Explanations — Module 7

**7.1**
1. **Zero work** — no displacement occurs (wall doesn't move).
2. **Positive work** — gravity acts in the same direction as the falling motion.
3. **Zero work** (by the lifting force) — the force holding the tray up is vertical while the displacement is horizontal (perpendicular ⇒ cos90°=0).
4. **Positive work** — force and displacement are in the same direction.
5. **Positive work** — assuming the push causes displacement in the direction of the push.

**7.2**
1. **Positive work**
2. **Positive work**
3. **Positive work**
4. **Negative work** — the person's hands push upward (opposing gravity) but the object still moves down, so force and displacement are in opposite directions.
5. **Negative work** — friction acts opposite to the block's motion (leftward), while the block moves right.

**7.3** W = Fd cosθ = (10)(5)cos(0°) = **50 J**

**7.4** Since the block moves at **constant speed**, ΔKE = 0, so by the Work-Energy Theorem: **W_net = 0 J** (even though individual forces like the applied force and friction each do nonzero work, they cancel out).

**7.5** W = F⃗·d⃗ = (3)(6) + (−1)(12) + (11)(1) = 18 − 12 + 11 = **17 J**

**7.6** Frictionless + constant speed → net force = 0 → **W_net = 0 J**

**7.7** Constant speed upward means the applied force balances gravity; W = Fd cos(0°) = (20)(5) = **100 J**

**7.8**
- Work lifting up the stairs: W1 = Fd = (200 N)(10.0 m) = 2,000 J
- Work pushing horizontally: W2 = Fd = (50.0 N)(35.0 m) = 1,750 J
- **Total work = 2,000 + 1,750 = 3,750 J**

**7.9** A satellite in circular orbit has its (centripetal) force always **perpendicular** to its velocity/displacement direction (cos90° = 0). **Work done = 0 J**

**7.10**
- W_tension = Fd cosθ = (200)(100)cos30° ≈ 200×100×0.866 = **17,320 J**
- W_friction = −fd (friction opposes motion) = −(80)(100) = **−8,000 J**
- W_net = W_tension + W_friction (gravity & normal force do 0 J, being perpendicular to motion) = 17,320 − 8,000 = **9,320 J**

**7.11** *(Graph-based):* Work in each interval equals the **area under the Force vs. Displacement graph** between those two displacement values (positive area = positive work; area below the axis = negative work). Add up the individual interval areas to get the total work (g).

**7.12** *(Diagram-based; PE ∝ height when mass is constant):* Since PEgrav = mgh, the PE at any lower position equals 50 J × (height of that position ÷ height of the top platform). Read the relative heights of each labeled step/position from your class diagram and scale accordingly.

**7.13** PE = mgh = (3.0)(9.8)(0.45) = **13.23 J**

**7.14**
- (a) **Both do the same amount of work** — work does not depend on time, only on force and displacement (same barbell, same height, same number of reps for both).
- (b) **Ben delivers more power** — since P = W/t, and Ben does the same work in less time (10 s vs. 60 s).

**7.15** Let Jill's work = W and time = t. Since Jack is twice as massive and lifts through the same height, his work = 2W. Since Jill takes half the time, Jack's time = 2t.
- Jill's power = W/t
- Jack's power = 2W/2t = W/t
- **They deliver the same power!** (A classic result: despite doing twice the work, Jack takes twice as long, so the power output is equal.)

**7.16** P = W/t = 0.50 J / 2 s = **0.25 W**

## MODULE 8 — Impulse and Momentum

**Q8.1** Determine the momentum of: (a) a 60-kg halfback moving eastward at 9 m/s; (b) a 1,000-kg car moving northward at 20 m/s; (c) a 40-kg freshman moving southward at 2 m/s.

**Q8.2** A car possesses 20,000 units of momentum. What is its new momentum if: (a) velocity is doubled; (b) velocity is tripled; (c) mass is doubled; (d) both velocity and mass are doubled?

**Q8.3** Consider a tennis ball colliding with a wall (per the figure/graph in your slides). Which scenario has the greatest velocity change? Greatest acceleration? Greatest momentum change? Greatest impulse?

**Q8.4** Jimmy Neutron's car and Cindy Vortex's truck have a head-on collision. (a) Which vehicle experiences the greatest force of impact? (b) Greatest impulse? (c) Greatest momentum change? (d) Greatest acceleration?

**Q8.5** Ultra Lord and Sheen launch a missile from a mobile launcher (neglect friction). Using momentum conservation, describe what happens to the launcher and the missile.

**Q8.6** Two manned satellites approach one another at a relative speed of 0.250 m/s, intending to dock. The first has a mass of 4×10³ kg, the second 7.5×10³ kg. If the two satellites collide **elastically** rather than dock, what is their final relative velocity?

**Q8.7** A truck and car are about to collide head-on. (a) Which experiences greater force? (b) Greater impulse? (c) Greater change in momentum? (d) Greater change in velocity?

### Answer Key & Explanations — Module 8

**8.1**
- (a) p = mv = 60×9 = **540 kg·m/s, East**
- (b) p = 1,000×20 = **20,000 kg·m/s, North**
- (c) p = 40×2 = **80 kg·m/s, South**

**8.2**
- (a) doubling v → p_new = 2×20,000 = **40,000 units**
- (b) tripling v → p_new = 3×20,000 = **60,000 units**
- (c) doubling m → p_new = 2×20,000 = **40,000 units**
- (d) doubling both m and v → p_new = 2×2×20,000 = **80,000 units**

**8.3** Since Δp = mΔv, J = Δp, and a = Δv/Δt: **whichever scenario shows the greatest change in velocity over the shortest time will have the greatest values for all four quantities** — velocity change, acceleration, momentum change, and impulse are all directly linked.

**8.4**
- (a) **Both vehicles experience the same magnitude of force** — this is a Newton's 3rd Law action-reaction pair.
- (b) **Both experience the same impulse** (equal force acting over the equal contact time).
- (c) **Both experience the same magnitude of momentum change** (momentum lost by one = momentum gained by the other).
- (d) **The car (less massive vehicle) experiences the greater acceleration** — since a = F/m, and F is the same for both, the smaller mass yields a bigger acceleration.

**8.5** *(Conceptual, conservation of momentum):* Before firing, total momentum = 0 (system at rest). After firing, the missile moves forward with momentum +p; by conservation of momentum, the **launcher must recoil backward with momentum −p**, so that the total momentum remains zero.

**8.6** A key property of **elastic collisions**: the **relative velocity of separation equals the relative velocity of approach** (same magnitude, reversed). Since the satellites approached at 0.250 m/s, their **final relative velocity after an elastic collision = 0.250 m/s** (now separating).

**8.7**
- (a) **Same force** for both (Newton's 3rd Law)
- (b) **Same impulse** for both
- (c) **Same momentum change** for both
- (d) **The lighter vehicle (car) has the greater change in velocity**, since Δv = Δp/m and Δp is equal for both, but the car has less mass.

**10.2** *(Method — angles from your class figure needed):* Apply both equilibrium conditions:
- ΣFₓ = 0: T₁cosθ₁ (in one direction) = T₂cosθ₂ (in the other)

Solve these two equations simultaneously for T₁ and T₂ using the specific angles shown in your figure.

**10.3**
- Perpendicular force, lever arm = 0.2 m: Torque = Force × lever arm = (120 N)(0.2 m) = **24 N·m**
